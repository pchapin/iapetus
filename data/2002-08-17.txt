
Evening, August 17, 2002

Tonight I attempted to observe the near earth asteroid (NEA) 2002 NY40.
Jeff posted a message about this object earlier. It was going to be
passing closest to the Earth this weekend, just a little ways beyond the
moon's orbit.

I used an ephemeris generator at JPL (there was a link to it on the page
Jeff referenced in his earlier posting) to compute an ephemeris for this
object. (*NOTE* An ephemeris is a table showing the position of an
object as a function of time. Often other computed information about the
object, such as estimated brightness and distance are also shown).

Since the object was going to be close and thus moving "quickly" I
configured the ephemeris generator to give me data each hour for one day
interval on either side of Aug 18, 0h00m UT (tonight at 8:00pm). I also
gave the ephemeris generator my location on the earth rather than
letting it compute a geocentric ephemeris. For such a nearby object, the
radius of the earth is significant enough to make a difference.

I plotted the movement of 2002 NY40 on the AAVSO star atlas. I used this
atlas because it is the "deepest" I own... showing stars to about
magnitude 9. It is also the most detailed. I was impressed at how
rapidly the object is crossing the sky. Most asteroids take days to
travel a few degrees. This thing is moving more than 10 degrees in just
a couple of hours. I noticed that at 10:00pm tonight the object would be
only a short distance from a magnitude 5.4 star that I figured I could
find easily. The close proximity to that star would also make 2002 NY40
easier to identify since even a small motion could be easily seen with
such a nearby reference.

I set up outside around 9:45pm to give myself some time to learn the
star field (in eastern Lyr... very rich and somewhat difficult). I had
WWV playing on a radio in the background so I wouldn't loose track of
time. Alas, I quickly realized, as I had feared, that my binoculars were
not up to the task. Although I was able to see deeper than 8.2 with them
(perhaps all the way to 9), the ephemeris was estimating 2002 NY40 at
magnitude 9.8+. I believe that was beyond my reach. I had hoped that the
motion would be so rapid that I would be able to notice it in binoculars
in only a minute or so. As it was I could not identify anything that
looked like 2002 NY40.

I suspect that I would have had more luck with my telescope. I had the
field down tightly enough that I could have easily surveyed it with the
'scope and the rapid motion would have shown up there readily. I suspect
that I could have been able to even see it moving... that would have
been cool. I'm sorry I didn't set that up.

Anyway, it was an interesting attempt at least. Formal sky conditions
follow.

Peter
